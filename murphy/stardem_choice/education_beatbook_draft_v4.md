## Talbot County

### Top Three Issues on the Education Beat

### Board Turnover and Political Realignment  

The Talbot County Board of Education has undergone a rapid reshuffle since November 2024, with three incumbents voted out, three newcomers sworn in, and a new president, Emily Jackson, taking the helm.  The election coincided with a statewide surge of conservative candidates backed by the 1776 Project PAC, a development that could steer district policy away from the progressive mandates of the Blueprint for Maryland’s Future.  While the incoming members have pledged continuity on core academic goals, their differing political philosophies are already prompting heated debates over curriculum choices, school‑choice proposals, and the level of parental control in classrooms.  

Historically, board composition has already proved pivotal: the 2023 resignation of Susan Delean‑Botkin and the subsequent appointment of Anna Howie occurred amid early Blueprint rollout challenges.  Those shifts set a precedent for how board dynamics directly affect implementation timelines and oversight of major contracts, a pattern that reporters should monitor as the new slate navigates the 2025‑26 budget and calendar approvals.

### Funding the Blueprint: Budget Battles and Facility Upgrades  

Talbot County’s fiscal agenda is dominated by the need to fund the Blueprint for Maryland’s Future while addressing a surge in enrollment that strains aging facilities.  In July 2025 CFO Sarah Jones unveiled a $12 million strategic plan to modernize school buildings and expand STEM programming, a proposal that will be weighed against the initial 2025‑26 budget presented in January 2025.  The board’s work sessions have highlighted a tug‑of‑war between allocating resources for staffing, technology, and the extensive renovation of sites such as Chapel District Elementary, whose design plans were sent to the state for review in late 2023.  

The tension is amplified by state‑level pressures: Maryland Comptroller Brooke Lierman’s call for a new student‑need counting system could reshape eligibility for Blueprint aid, while Governor Wes Moore’s administration is watching local compliance closely.  Reporters should track how the board reconciles these external mandates with community expectations for safe, modern learning environments.

### Student Achievement Gaps and Equity Pressures  

Student performance and equity have surfaced as urgent concerns, most visibly in the December 2024 Maryland School Report Card downgrade for Easton Middle School and the revelation that over half of Talbot County students qualify for free or reduced‑price lunches.  District officials cite the data as a catalyst for expanding meal access under Senate Bill 579 and for launching targeted interventions aimed at improving attendance and test scores under the Maryland Comprehensive Assessment Program.  

These challenges are not new; the 2023 lawsuit accusing social‑media firms of harming student mental health and the 2023 “Contracted Out” spending analysis underscored systemic strains on resources and oversight.  The current board’s promise to implement corrective actions and the superintendent’s focus on STEM and targeted support programs indicate a strategic pivot, but the effectiveness of those measures will be measured in the next state report card and in the lived experience of students facing economic hardship.

## Kent County

### Top Three Issues on the Education Beat

### Blueprint Funding Crunch  

Recent testimony from Eastern Shore legislators—including Delegates Steven Arentz, Sheree Sample‑Hughes, and Chris Adams—has sharpened the focus on the financial strain the Blueprint for Maryland’s Future places on Kent County. Their February 2025 briefing warned that the state’s cost‑sharing model forces counties to shoulder billions in construction, technology, and staffing upgrades, a burden that the current General Assembly budget simply cannot absorb. The same budget discussion resurfaced in the 2025 legislative session where Delegates Jay Jacobs, Tom Hutchinson, and Jefferson Ghrist earmarked the Blueprint alongside the state budget and energy policy as top priorities, pressing for increased state aid and a revision of the cost‑sharing formula.

Kent County Board President Joe Goetz and Superintendent Mary Boswell‑McComas have echoed these concerns, noting that the district’s FY25 budget already stretches thin to meet Blueprint mandates while preserving essential programs. The lingering dispute over the Cade funding formula—raised in a 2024 delegation to Governor Wes Moore—adds another layer of uncertainty for community‑college pipelines that feed the K‑12 system. Without a clear infusion of state resources, Kent County risks delaying critical facility upgrades and technology rollouts outlined in its Facilities Strategic Plan.

The funding impasse is shaping every other education story on the Eastern Shore. As legislators lobby for a revised formula, district leaders are forced to prioritize short‑term fixes over long‑term investments, a tension that will dominate board meetings and community forums throughout the coming school year.

### Closing Pandemic‑Induced Learning Gaps  

The March 2025 briefing by Superintendents Patricia Saelens (Queen Anne’s County) and Mary Boswell‑McComas highlighted that Kent County is still wrestling with the academic fallout of COVID‑19. Deploying Blueprint resources, the district has expanded tutoring, added supplemental school‑day hours, and overhauled curricula to target reading and math deficits that have persisted despite a modest rebound in statewide MCAP scores. The 2025 State of Reading Report, released in April, underscored a decade‑long decline in reading proficiency, prompting the Maryland Department of Education to push districts for immediate intervention plans.

Kent’s latest MCAP results from August 2025 showed the district holding steady—neither the gains seen in Dorchester nor the declines in Caroline—signaling that existing remediation efforts are holding the line but not yet moving the needle. Superintendent Boswell‑McComas has stressed that the Blueprint’s flexible funding streams are essential for scaling proven programs, yet the same financial constraints flagged in the funding debate threaten to curtail the depth of these interventions.

For reporters, the tension between ambitious Blueprint‑driven remediation and the fiscal reality of county budgets creates a fertile beat: school‑board minutes, parent‑teacher forums, and state‑level policy debates will reveal whether Kent can close the learning gaps before the next assessment cycle.

### Student Homelessness and Mental‑Health Crisis  

July 2025 data released by Kent and Talbot County schools revealed a 30 % surge in student homelessness, a trend that district officials say is straining already limited support services. Superintendent Boswell‑McComas and spokesperson Dan Divilio have called on the Maryland State Department of Education to enforce the McKinney‑Vento Act more rigorously and to allocate emergency resources for shelter, transportation, and counseling. The rise mirrors a broader statewide concern about basic‑needs insecurity, which has been compounded by a sudden budget shortfall that forced the General Assembly to slash much of the $45 million youth‑mental‑health funding earmarked by Governor Wes Moore earlier in the year.

The budget cut has left programs like Thrive Behavioral Health operating at reduced capacity just as demand spikes, prompting local NGOs and the Kent County Chamber of Commerce to step in with ad‑hoc assistance. While Open Doors Partners in Education’s February 2025 literacy initiative offers a positive example of community collaboration, it also highlights the gap between targeted grants and the systemic needs of students facing housing instability and mental‑health challenges.

Coverage of this issue will need to track how district leaders balance immediate emergency response with longer‑term policy advocacy, and how state legislators respond to the mounting pressure to protect vulnerable students amid fiscal turbulence.

## Dorchester County

### Funding Gaps Threaten Blueprint Implementation  

Recent board briefings reveal a widening fiscal chasm between Dorchester County’s projected Blueprint for Maryland’s Future expenses and the money actually available. In late January 2025 Superintendent Jymil Thompson warned the Board of Education that a budget deficit could force the district to scale back staffing, renovation plans, and several Blueprint‑linked initiatives. The deficit narrative was reinforced in March when County Council President Lenny Pfeffer announced that the Blueprint’s cost curve now exceeds the 2022 estimate, citing rising construction and personnel expenses.  

State‑level pressure is mounting as Eastern Shore legislators, including Delegate Sheree Sample‑Hughes, met in February to argue that the Blueprint’s cost‑sharing formula places unsustainable burdens on county coffers. Their push for increased state aid and a recalibrated funding formula underscores a broader regional concern that the Blueprint, while educationally ambitious, may outpace local revenue streams without legislative relief.  

The funding strain is not a new story; a 2023‑24 budget cycle already highlighted the delicate balance between Blueprint mandates and local tax capacity. However, the recent convergence of a declared deficit, higher‑than‑expected construction costs, and vocal legislative criticism signals that fiscal sustainability will dominate the district’s agenda for the foreseeable future.

### Aging Facilities Spark Capital Review  

On November 23 2024 Dorchester County Public Schools announced an audit of its oldest elementary buildings after teachers Karen McGowan and Christy Starling demanded urgent upgrades. Assistant Superintendent Michael Collins was tasked with coordinating the review, linking it to state‑level Built‑To‑Learn and Capital Improvement programs. The move reflects growing anxiety that deteriorating infrastructure hampers both student safety and instructional quality.  

The facilities issue dovetails with the broader Blueprint financing debate, as the district now faces higher construction estimates than originally projected. Historically, the county’s capital‑improvement challenges have been tied to the Blueprint’s ambitious building‑replacement goals, a tension that resurfaced in the 2023‑24 budget discussions. The current audit could determine whether the district must re‑prioritize projects, seek additional state aid, or explore alternative financing mechanisms to keep schools operational and up to code.  

Stakeholders—including board members, the Maryland Interagency Commission, and community advocates—are watching the audit’s findings closely, aware that any delay in addressing aging structures could trigger compliance issues with state safety standards and further strain an already tight budget.

### At‑Risk Student Services Face Program Cuts  

The district’s commitment to alternative education and intensive support for at‑risk youth hit a setback on November 23 2024 when DCPS announced it would not move forward with a proposed alternative education program, citing limited enrollment and budget constraints. Superintendent Thompson and Board member Chris Wheedleton framed the decision as fiscally responsible, yet the move sparked concern among educators who see such programs as essential for keeping vulnerable students engaged.  

Just weeks later, the Board voted to retain staffing at Positive Change Academy for the 2024‑25 school year, a decision championed by PASS Educators founder Ronnell Page and Academy principal Dane Harris. While the academy’s continued operation offers a lifeline for some at‑risk students, the broader cancellation of the new alternative program signals a narrowing of options at a time when the district is still grappling with post‑pandemic learning gaps.  

Historically, Dorchester has wrestled with providing adequate support for struggling learners, a theme evident in the 2023‑24 proficiency gains tied to targeted accountability measures. The current tension between budget realities and the need for specialized programming underscores a critical beat: reporters must track how funding constraints reshape the safety net for the district’s most vulnerable students.

Historically, the district has leaned on state funding to drive large‑scale projects—from the 2023 energy‑savings initiative that freed cash for classrooms to the 2023 budget priorities that emphasized Blueprint spending. ### Student Engagement, Discipline, and Data Security

These twin challenges—maintaining classroom order while safeguarding digital privacy—highlight the district’s need to integrate technology policies with robust cybersecurity measures. The experience builds on earlier efforts, such as the 2023 summer school expansion funded through ARP ESSER, showing a pattern of leveraging external resources to bolster both academic outcomes and operational resilience.

## Queen Anne's County

### Top Three Issues on the Education Beat

### Funding Gaps and the Blueprint for Maryland’s Future  

Recent weeks have seen Eastern Shore legislators—Steven Arentz, Sheree Sample‑Hughes, and Chris Adams—press the General Assembly for more state aid, warning that the Blueprint’s cost‑sharing formula is draining county budgets. Their February 2025 briefing echoed earlier concerns voiced at the 2025 legislative session, where delegates highlighted the Blueprint, the state budget, and energy policy as the trio of top‑tier challenges for school financing. The urgency is heightened by a February 1, 2025 meeting with Sen. Van Hollen, where a federal grant pause threatened to stall Blueprint projects and the Sellinger Grant Program, leaving counties to scramble for interim cash.  

County leaders, including Queen Anne’s County Commissioners, have responded by reallocating resources—shifting Chesapeake College’s technology‑center costs to county funds in April 2025—to keep Blueprint‑related initiatives afloat. While these maneuvers provide short‑term relief, they underscore a structural mismatch between the statewide Blueprint mandates and local revenue capacity, a tension that first surfaced in 2023 when Caroline County officials balked at a $94 million building‑replacement share.  

For reporters, the story line is clear: every budget discussion, grant negotiation, or county‑level cost shift is now filtered through the prism of Blueprint funding. Tracking how legislators, the Board of Education, and the State Department of Education negotiate the fiscal shortfall will illuminate the next wave of school‑building, staffing, and program decisions in Queen Anne’s County.

### Closing Learning Gaps and the Reading Decline  

Superintendents Patricia Saelens and Mary Boswell‑McComas convened in March 2025 to deploy Blueprint resources toward pandemic‑induced learning gaps, expanding tutoring, extending the school day, and revising curricula. Their push comes as the Maryland READS‑backed 2025 State of Reading Report confirmed a decade‑long slide in reading proficiency, prompting an urgent call for interventions anchored in Blueprint funding.  

The same pressure is reflected in the August 2025 MCAP releases, which showed mixed results across the Shore—modest gains in Dorchester, a dip in Caroline, and flat performance in Kent—reinforcing the need for targeted remediation. Historical context shows this is not a new battle; the 2023 MCAP results had already highlighted recovery from pandemic loss but warned of lingering science declines, while early‑2024 READS presentations urged the State Board to adopt data‑driven literacy strategies.  

Beat coverage should follow how Queen Anne’s County translates these statewide diagnostics into concrete classroom actions—whether through expanded literacy blocks, new reading specialists, or partnerships with community organizations—and monitor any measurable shifts in proficiency as the next state assessment cycle approaches.

### Governance Tensions – Charter Schools and School‑Board Politics

This back‑and‑forth illustrates a broader governance tug‑of‑war that intensified after the November 2024 school‑board elections, where conservatives, backed by the 1776 Project PAC, captured multiple seats and signaled a shift toward greater parental control.  

These political currents intersect with longstanding procedural debates—such as the 2024 Board’s approval of Sysco food contracts and piggybacked procurement moves—that reflect how the board balances fiscal stewardship with educational vision. The recent charter decision, coupled with the conservative board gains, signals a new era where policy direction, curriculum standards, and community expectations are being renegotiated in real time.  

For reporters, the beat now follows a dual narrative: the concrete outcomes of charter applications and the broader ideological realignment of the board. Monitoring board meeting minutes, appeal filings, and stakeholder interviews will reveal how these governance battles shape the educational landscape for Queen Anne’s County families.

## Key Documents, Records & Data Sources to Track (All Counties)

### Budget & Finance Records  
- **Annual FY Budget Packages (FY 2024‑2027)** – Detailed line‑item spending, revenue sources, and Blueprint funding allocations; posted on each County Board of Education website (e.g., talbotpublicschools.org → Budget → Fiscal Year).  
- **Quarterly Financial Statements & Audits** – Balance‑sheet, income‑statement, and audit findings for the school system; available through the County Board of Education “Financial Reports” page and the Maryland State Comptroller’s Transparency Portal.  
- **Cade Funding Formula Reports** – Annual allocations to community colleges and K‑12 districts under the Cade formula; published by the Maryland State Department of Education (MSDE) Funding Division and downloadable from msde.maryland.gov → Finance → Cade.  
- **Blueprint for Maryland’s Future Funding Tracker** – County‑level disbursement schedules, grant‑level breakdowns, and cost‑share calculations; found on the MSDE Blueprint Accountability & Implementation Board (AIB) website (blueprint.maryland.gov → Funding Tracker).  
- **School Construction & Capital Improvement Budgets** – Projected costs, bond‑issue details, and capital‑project status reports; posted on County Board of Education “Facilities” pages and the Maryland Interagency Commission on School Construction portal.  
- **Board of Public Works Appropriations Orders** – State‑level appropriations that affect county school funding; accessible via the Maryland General Assembly website (mgaleg.maryland.gov → Board of Public Works Orders).  
- **Free & Reduced‑Price Lunch (FRPL) Eligibility Reports** – Student‑level subsidy counts used for state aid calculations; released each spring by the County Office of Nutrition Services and aggregated on the Maryland Department of Education FRPL dashboard.  
- **Grant Award Letters & Expenditure Reports** – Documentation for Maryland Sellinger Grants, Excellence in Maryland Public Schools Act awards, and other federal/state grants; typically posted in the County “Grants” section or obtained via FOIA requests to the County Office of Grants Management.  

### Assessment & Academic Data  
- **Maryland Comprehensive Assessment Program (MCAP) Results** – Student performance by grade, subject, and subgroup; downloadable from the MSDE “Assessments” portal (msde.maryland.gov → Assessments → MCAP).  
- **Maryland School Report Card (MSRC)** – School‑level accountability scores, growth metrics, and attendance data; viewable on the Maryland State Data Center website (datacenter.maryland.gov → School Report Card).  
- **Blueprint for Maryland’s Future Accountability Reports** – Progress on equity, teacher recruitment, and learning‑gap reduction; released quarterly on the Blueprint AIB site.  
- **Student Demographics & Enrollment Data** – Counts by race/ethnicity, special‑education status, English‑learner status, and mobility; found on each County’s “Data & Accountability” dashboard and the Maryland State Data Center.  
- **Learning‑Loss Recovery Metrics** – Post‑pandemic remediation data, tutoring hours, and extended‑day usage; compiled by the County Office of Academic Services and posted in annual “Recovery Reports.”  
- **Special Education Outcome Reports** – IEP compliance, placement, and achievement data; available through the County Special Education Office and the MSDE Special Education Data System.  
- **School Climate & Safety Survey Results** – Student/parent/teacher perceptions of safety, bullying, and mental‑health resources; published after each biennial survey on County Board of Education websites.  

### Personnel Records  
- **Superintendent & Board of Education Contracts** – Salary, benefits, performance metrics, and contract length; posted on the County Board of Education “Leadership” page.  
- **Collective Bargaining Agreements (CBAs)** – Terms for teachers, support staff, and administrators; accessible via the County Human Resources portal and the Maryland State Education Association (MSEA) website.  
- **Employee Payroll & Benefit Summaries** – Annual payroll totals, overtime, and benefit cost breakdowns; part of the FY Budget Package and also filed with the Maryland Department of Labor.  
- **Teacher Certification & Licensure Database** – Status of certifications, endorsements, and reciprocity; searchable through the Maryland State Department of Education “Educator Licensing” portal.  
- **Staff Turnover & Vacancy Reports** – Year‑over‑year vacancy rates, recruitment pipelines, and turnover reasons; released each summer by County HR Offices.  
- **Professional Development Records** – Attendance, funding, and outcomes of Blueprint‑mandated PD (e.g., STEM, SEL, equity training); posted in the County “Professional Development” section.  

### Facilities & Capital Planning  
- **School Construction Project Plans & Renderings** – Architectural drawings, timelines, and contractor information for renovations (e.g., Chapel District Elementary); available on the County Board of Education “Facilities Projects” page and the Interagency Commission on School Construction site.  
- **Facilities Condition Assessments** – Scores for HVAC, roofing, safety, and ADA compliance; published annually in the “Facilities Report.”  
- **Capital Improvement Bond Ordinances & Voter Referenda** – Text of bond measures, election results, and financing terms; filed with the County Council and accessible via the County “Election Results” archive.  
- **Energy‑Efficiency and Sustainability Audits** – Findings on utility usage, solar installations, and green‑building certifications; posted on County “Sustainability” dashboards.  
- **Emergency Preparedness & Active Shooter Training Records** – Training dates, participant lists, and after‑action reports; maintained by the County Office of Safety & Security and often posted after board approval.  

### Governance & Board Documents  
- **Board of Education Meeting Agendas, Minutes, and Recordings** – Full agenda items, decisions, and video recordings for each monthly meeting; hosted on the County Board of Education “Board Meetings” portal.  
- **Board of Education Policy Manuals & Bylaws** – Governing policies on curriculum, student conduct, procurement, and equity; downloadable from the Board’s “Policies” page.  
- **Special Education Committee Charters & Minutes** – Charters, meeting minutes, and action items related to special‑education oversight; available under the Board’s “Committees” section.  
- **Board of Education Election Results & Candidate Filings** – Certified results, candidate statements, and campaign finance disclosures; posted on the Maryland State Board of Elections website and County election archives.  
- **School Board Election Campaign Finance Reports** – Contributions, expenditures, and donor information for board races; searchable through the Maryland Board of Elections campaign finance portal.  

### Student Services & Equity Data  
- **Free & Reduced‑Meal Eligibility Trends** – Annual FRPL participation rates by school; published by County Nutrition Services and the Maryland Department of Education FRPL Dashboard.  
- **McKinney‑Vento Homelessness Counts** – Number of students experiencing homelessness and services provided; reported each fall to the County Office of Student Services and uploaded to the State Homelessness Data System.  
- **English Learner (EL) Progress Reports** – EL proficiency growth, program placement, and staffing; posted on County “EL Services” pages.  
- **School Discipline & Disparities Reports** – Suspension, expulsion, and disciplinary action data broken down by race and disability; released annually as part of the State’s School Discipline Transparency Initiative.  
- **School Health & Immunization Records** – Aggregate vaccination rates and health‑clinic utilization; available through the County Health Department’s “School Health” portal.  

### Legal & Policy Documents  
- **State Education statutes & Regulations** – Full text of the Blueprint for Maryland’s Future, No Distracted Learning Act, and Excellence in Maryland Public Schools Act; accessible via the Maryland General Assembly’s “Legislation” site (legis.state.md.us).  
- **Maryland Department of Education Guidance Letters** – Implementation guidance on assessment changes, curriculum standards, and funding formulas; posted on msde.maryland.gov → Guidance.  
- **State Inspector General for Education Audit Reports** – Audits of procurement, contract compliance, and program performance; downloadable from the Office of the Inspector General website.  
- **Office of the Attorney General Opinions on School‑Related Legal Issues** – Opinions on student privacy, immigration enforcement, and school‑resource officer policies; available on the AG’s website.  

### Grants, Funding Programs & Legislative Resources

- **Kirwan Mandates Implementation Reports** – County compliance with mandated reforms (e.g., teacher evaluation, curriculum alignment); published on the Blueprint AIB site.  
- **Legislative Budget Hearings Transcripts & Testimony** – Testimony from county superintendents, CFOs, and legislators on education funding; archived on the Maryland General Assembly’s “Committee Hearings” page.

### Data Dashboards & Interactive Portals
